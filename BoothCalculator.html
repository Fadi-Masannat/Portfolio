The Process



What is Booth's Multiplier?


Booth's multiplier represents a sophisticated algorithm designed to streamline the process of binary multiplication, known for its efficiency in hardware implementations. By harnessing the principles learned in my CPRE 281 class, I delved into understanding Booth's algorithm, a technique that optimizes the multiplication process by reducing the number of partial products required. In essence, Booth's algorithm achieves this by examining pairs of adjacent bits in the multiplier, allowing for simplified addition and subtraction operations based on specific bit patterns. Integrating this algorithm into the functionality of the Altera DE2 board demanded meticulous planning and execution. However, the result was a robust multiplication calculator capable of handling binary multiplication tasks swiftly and accurately. This project not only expanded my technical prowess but also underscored the gratification derived from conquering complex challenges in digital design and implementation.






Top-Level Diagram:


The top-level diagram illustrates the architecture of a comprehensive digital circuit designed to perform Booth's multiplication. It features inputs for two 8-bit numbers, labeled as Top[7..0] and Mult[7..0], along with control signals such as RegSel1 and RegSel0 for selecting specific registers. Additionally, there are clock inputs, ClockRegisters and ClockShift, responsible for coordinating the timing of register loading and multiplication operations. A select input, SelectHex, allows for switching between displaying the right and left halves of the 16-bit result on the hex displays. The circuit comprises several essential modules, including 32 1-bit registers, 8 2-to-1 multiplexers, 2 seven-segment decoders, a 2-to-4 decoder, and a ShiftAdd module. The workflow begins with loading the two input numbers into registers, followed by pulsing ClockShift to initiate multiplication. The resulting 16-bit product is then stored in specific registers, indicated by select signals 10 and 11. Finally, the calculated result is displayed on hex displays 1 and 0, offering visual feedback to the user. Despite encountering challenges with the state machine implementation, the circuit demonstrates robust functionality and computational prowess in performing Booth's multiplication efficiently.
